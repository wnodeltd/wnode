# Identity and Attribution

## Overview
Identity and attribution form the **economic and operational backbone** of the wnode network.  
They determine:

- Who owns what,
- Who earns what,
- Which founder tree a nodlr belongs to,
- Which nodlr a node belongs to,
- How override flows,
- How compute revenue is attributed,
- How the steward enforces fairness and prevents manipulation.

Identity is **immutable**.  
Attribution is **deterministic**.  
Transparency is **constitutional**.

This document defines how identity is created, how attribution works, and how the steward protects the integrity of the entire economic structure.

---

# Identity Model

Identity in the network is composed of three layers:

1. **Founder identity**  
2. **Nodlr identity**  
3. **Node identity**

Each layer is permanent, immutable, and bound to the next.

---

# Founder Identity

### Fixed Supply: 10 Founder Accounts
There are **exactly 10 founder accounts**:

- **5 active**
- **5 inactive**

Of the 5 active founders:

- **2 are assigned to real individuals**
- **3 are unassigned but active and accruing override**

Unassigned founders:

- Still accrue override,
- Still receive sequential nodlrs,
- Still grow their trees,
- Are strategic leverage for developers, investors, and future steward license holders.

Inactive founders:

- Do not accrue override,
- Do not receive nodlrs,
- Can be activated at any time.

### Immutable 3% Override
Every founder account receives a **fixed, immutable 3% override** on all compute revenue generated by its tree.

This 3%:

- Cannot be changed,
- Cannot be reduced,
- Cannot be increased,
- Cannot be disabled,
- Cannot be reallocated,
- Cannot be modified by the steward or any future owner.

It is a **perpetual economic right**, identical in permanence to nodlr account ownership.

---

# Nodlr Identity

### Permanent and Immutable
A nodlr identity:

- Is created once,
- Cannot be edited,
- Cannot be transferred,
- Cannot be deleted,
- Cannot be reassigned to another founder.

Nodlrs are the **economic operators** of nodes.

### Founder Tree Assignment
A nodlr joins a founder tree in one of two ways:

1. **Without invite code**  
   - They are placed sequentially under one of the 5 active founders.

2. **With invite code**  
   - They join under the nodlr who invited them, inheriting that founder’s tree.

Nodlrs **cannot**:

- Move between founders,
- Change their tree,
- Reassign their lineage.

Tree membership is **permanent**.

---

# Node Identity

### One‑Machine‑One‑Node
Each node is bound to:

- A unique Node ID,
- A unique hardware fingerprint,
- A unique nodlr identity.

Nodes cannot:

- Change nodlr ownership,
- Change founder tree,
- Clone identity,
- Spoof identity.

### Immutable Node Identity
Once a node is registered:

- Its identity is permanent,
- Its lineage is permanent,
- Its attribution path is permanent.

Nodes inherit their economic lineage from their nodlr.

---

# Attribution Model

Attribution determines:

- Which nodlr earns revenue,
- Which founder earns override,
- Which tree receives economic flow.

Attribution is:

- Deterministic,
- Immutable,
- Transparent,
- Auditable.

### Attribution Flow

### What Attribution Controls
Attribution determines:

- Node earnings,
- Nodlr earnings,
- Founder override,
- Tree‑local revenue flow.

### What Attribution Does NOT Control
Attribution does **not** influence:

- Routing,
- Compute class,
- Performance scoring,
- Confidentiality,
- Locality.

# Anti‑Manipulation Protections

The steward enforces:

- Immutable founder trees,
- Immutable nodlr placement,
- Immutable node identity,
- Hardware fingerprint binding,
- One‑machine‑one‑node,
- Update compliance.

This prevents:

- Identity laundering,
- Attribution fraud,
- Tree manipulation,
- Node impersonation,
- Cross‑tree contamination.

Founders cannot:

- Move nodlrs,
- Move nodes,
- Influence routing,
- Influence compute.

Nodlrs cannot:

- Change founder trees,
- Reassign nodes,
- Manipulate override.

Nodes cannot:

- Change nodlr identity,
- Change founder tree,
- Influence economic flows.
